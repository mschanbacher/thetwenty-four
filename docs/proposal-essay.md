## I. The problem

College football generates enough revenue to dictate how every other college sport is organized. It has dictated this for the last fifteen years, and the map we now have reflects that decision.

A conference exists to organize competition among its members. For most of the twentieth century this meant geography. Programs within a day's drive of one another played each other in the sports of the era — football in the fall, basketball in the winter, track and baseball in the spring, swimming and wrestling and tennis in their respective seasons — and a conference championship was, by construction, a regional honor. The Pacific-12's predecessor conferences date to 1915. The Southeastern Conference was formed in 1932. The Big Ten goes back further still, to 1896. These are century-old institutions organized around the practical question of which schools could reach each other.

Television changed this slowly, then quickly. Football's audience was always the largest of any college sport, and the rights to broadcast football games have been the largest line in any conference's budget for as long as there have been broadcast rights to sell. But for most of the television era, the gap between football's revenue and the cost of running everything else was manageable inside a conference built on regional lines. Michigan's football dollars supported Michigan's other sports. The conference's job was to administer competition; the market's job was to pay Michigan for its football games; those two jobs did not conflict.

They conflict now. The scale of the football rights market has grown to the point where the structure of a conference is no longer a decision a conference's members get to make independently of the market. The Big Ten's [2023 media deal is worth over a billion dollars a year](https://www.sportico.com/business/media/2022/big-ten-media-deal-1234686167/). The SEC [distributes about the same amount to its members annually from its combined revenue streams](https://www.secsports.com/news/2025/02/sec-announces-2023-2024-revenue-distribution). These figures are large enough to make any non-football consideration — travel, scheduling, the basic logistical question of whether volleyball teams can reasonably play a conference schedule — structurally secondary. A conference that declined to organize itself around maximizing football revenue would be leaving its members hundreds of millions of dollars a year on the table. No conference has declined. Some have missed out.

The result is the map we have. The Big Ten stretches from New Jersey, to California. The Southeastern Conference covers four time zones. Stanford, whose [36 varsity sports programs](https://facts.stanford.edu/campus-life/cardinal-athletics) are located in northern California, plays in the Atlantic Coast Conference. The athletic departments running these programs are not ignorant of the cost; they understand perfectly that their volleyball team flying to Chapel Hill for a match is absurd. They are making the trade anyway because the football revenue pays for the trip, and the football revenue is contingent on the conference structure that produces the trip.

The clearest demonstration of how the trade-off plays out is the Pac-12, a conference that had existed in one configuration or another since 1915 and was [effectively dissolved in a two-year span](https://www.espn.com/college-sports/story/_/id/38134021/oregon-washington-officially-leave-pac-12-big-ten). In the summer of 2022, UCLA and USC announced they were leaving for the Big Ten. In the summer of 2023, Colorado announced it was leaving for the Big 12, and Oregon and Washington followed UCLA and USC to the Big Ten; Utah and Arizona and Arizona State announced for the Big 12 soon after. By the end of the cycle, the twelve football-bearing programs of the Pac-12 had been scattered across three conferences, only one of them regional in any meaningful sense. The conference that remained held two programs — Oregon State and Washington State — which played a full football schedule under a scheduling alliance with the Mountain West and scheduled their other sports through cooperation with other leagues.

The cost of this landed almost entirely on people who had no role in making the decisions. A football coach might move with his program; a soccer player whose school had just joined the Big Ten did not get that option. A century of rivalries in sports other than football evaporated because the football rights market said they had to. Administrative departments that had spent decades organizing swim meets and gymnastics schedules on West Coast drives now had to plan for their athletes to fly to Rutgers. And the athletes themselves, whose seasons are structured around weekly competition and who generate almost none of the revenue that drove the realignment, absorbed the travel. The Pac-12's dissolution is the clearest case but it is not the only one. The Big 12 and the ACC were reshaped in the same window by the same forces, and the American Athletic Conference lost most of what identity it had accumulated. The conference map looks the way it does because football revenue shifted, and everything else was obliged to follow.

The [College Football Playoff's expansion to twelve teams](https://collegefootballplayoff.com/news/2022/12/1/cfp12-2425.aspx), announced in 2022 and first run in 2024, was sold as an answer to these pressures. The previous four-team playoff had, over its ten years, produced a field drawn almost entirely from the SEC, the Big Ten, and two or three other recurring contenders; the argument for expansion was that twelve teams would open access to the programs the four-team field had excluded. In practice, the expansion does no such thing. The twelve-team field reserves automatic bids for the five highest-ranked conference champions and distributes the other seven spots through the same selection committee that had run the four-team era. A program like Boise State, which went 12-1 as the Mountain West champion and earned the No. 3 seed by virtue of the autobid structure, lost its one playoff game to Penn State by a margin consistent with the pre-expansion expectation for a Group of Five program against a Power Four program. The expansion added seats at a table whose rules were unchanged. The programs that had been excluded under the four-team playoff remained structurally excluded under the twelve-team one; the committee was now debating the tenth and eleventh seeds rather than the third and fourth, but it was still the committee doing the debating, and the conferences whose television revenue underwrote the sport's economics were still providing most of the candidates.

The diagnosis that follows from these facts is structural. The conference system is being asked to organize competition across a set of sports whose economic scales differ by more than an order of magnitude, under market pressure that rewards optimizing for the largest one. The result is a map that makes sense for football's television market and makes sense for almost nothing else. No amount of tuning inside the current structure resolves the tension, because the tension is the structure. The Big Ten cannot both be a regional conference and maximize its media-deal value; it has chosen the latter, and every other sport it administers now pays the price. The College Football Playoff cannot both be a committee-selected field drawn from the largest conferences and a merit-based postseason open to the best teams in the country; it has chosen the former, and the teams outside those conferences play for a bid that does not exist.

A better arrangement is possible. It begins by separating the sport that generates the revenue from the structure that governs every other sport.

## II. The proposal, in outline

The proposal is a league of twenty-four. Membership is earned by finishing in the top twenty of last year's standings, or by winning it on the field in a Promotion Playoff. The league plays its own schedule, decides its own champion through the College Football Playoff, and turns over four teams a year. Everything else in college football — the regional conferences, their championships, their other sports — return to a stable state of existence.

The schedule is twelve games, because the regular season is twelve games. Ten are against other members of the Champions Conference. The remaining two are traditional rivalry games, which each team's home conference selects and which are allowed to be played against opponents outside the Champions Conference as well as inside. The Game survives — Ohio State and Michigan, both Champions Conference members, one in Division I and the other in Division II, meet every year regardless of the rest of the schedule. The Iron Bowl survives as well, even though Auburn is outside of the Conference at its creation. Alabama, which is inside, simply spends one of its rivalry slots on Auburn, and the game is played. The Egg Bowl survives on the same logic: Ole Miss is inside the Champions Conference and Mississippi State, at its inception, is not. These games are important enough that the proposal is built around preserving them. Ten-plus-two is the arithmetic that makes the preservation possible inside a twelve-game season.

What the arithmetic does not allow is a buy game. Under the current arrangement, most programs schedule at least one Saturday a year against an FCS opponent or a low-end Group of Five program; the athletic department writes a check, the visiting team takes the money, and the home team takes a win. Under the proposal, no Champions Conference team has a slot to do that. Every week on the calendar is a week against another Champions Conference program or a rivalry game chosen for its stature. The effect on strength of schedule is categorical: a Champions Conference season is designed to be exceptionally difficult to clear undefeated, and most teams in the league will finish with losses that currently only the sport's weakest Power Four teams accumulate. Currently, the country's best ten teams routinely finish 11–1 or 10–2, because they are playing ten different schedules of wildly different difficulty; the records look similar because the opponents don't. In the Champions Conference, the schedules are balanced by construction. The top teams play each other, and the losses have to land somewhere. A 10–2 season is rare. An 11–1 season is exceptional. Undefeated is nearly impossible. The tenth team in the standings might be 7–5. The record a team finishes with will mean something close to what the record says. No one will need to be the arbiter of strength of schedule.

The postseason is the top twelve of the standings and the bracket for the College Football Playoff remains untouched. Seeds one through four get byes, five through twelve play a first-round game on campus, and the bracket runs through the New Year's Six bowls to a national championship in mid-January. That is the whole selection process. There is no committee meeting, no strength-of-schedule argument, no debate about the twelfth seed. A team is where its record places it. The postseason is the decision the rest of the proposal serves: if the bracket is going to seed itself, the league has to be one whose standings can be read off directly, which means a league of known members playing a known schedule. That is what the Champions Conference is.

Membership turns over annually. The bottom two Champions Conference teams are relegated outright. The next two up play relegation games against teams coming out of a ten-team Promotion Playoff open to every FBS conference champion. Four teams change places each year, a turnover rate of roughly seventeen percent. The Premier League is the closest available precedent; it turns over [three of twenty](https://www.premierleague.com/en/premier-league-explained), around fifteen percent, and the evidence from its three decades under that system is that the rate is high enough for the bottom of the table to carry stakes every season and low enough for the league's identity to feel stable across years. The proposal tunes the number slightly higher because the candidate pool is larger and because college football tolerates roster churn better than a Premier League club does.

Outside the Champions Conference, the proposal assumes a return to the conference map as it stood around 2012. The Pac-12 reconstituted. Texas and Oklahoma back in the Big 12. The Big Ten no longer stretched from Piscataway to Los Angeles. Cal and Stanford return home to the Pacific Coast saying goodbye to the Atlantic. Once football revenue is no longer the organizing force of conference membership — because football revenue now follows the Champions Conference and not the conferences — conferences are free to serve their other sports, which is what they were built to do. The proposal rests on the 2012 map, and the reader is entitled to know that up front.

The inaugural twenty-four has to be selected by a method that is not itself merit-based, because there is no prior Champions Conference season to rank against. The top of the final CFP committee ranking is one method; a one-time selection committee is another; both yield roughly the same twenty-four. Any program that feels wrongly excluded from the initial field can earn its way in within one or two promotion cycles. This is the only part of the proposal that is not self-correcting on first principles, and it corrects itself in a handful of years. It is worth naming and not worth dwelling on.

What follows is each of these pieces in turn — the schedule, the promotion-and-relegation mechanism, the reimagined playoff, what the proposal preserves, what it gives up, and the questions that remain open. The proposal is not complete. It is complete enough to debate, iterate, and refine.

## III. How the Champions Conference works

Take a Champions Conference program — Oregon, say — and ask what it knows about its next season the day the previous one ends. By that evening, the program knows its twelve opponents. Not the dates, not the broadcast windows, not who will be ranked where come October, but the twelve teams it will play: seven from its division, three from the other two divisions, two rivalry games. The schedule is derived. The previous season's standings produced this one's divisions, the divisions produce the intra-division and cross-division games, and the program's home conference selects its rivalries. From the program's point of view, the season's architecture is settled before the offseason begins.

The league is arranged as three divisions of eight teams each. Divisions I, II, and III. They are not regional, and they are not permanent. At the end of every season, the standings are used to reconstitute the divisions for the next season through a snake draft — the top team in the league is placed in Division I, the second in II, the third in III, the fourth in III again, the fifth in II, the sixth in I, and so on through twenty-four. The pattern produces three divisions whose total strength, measured by previous-season record, is as close to equal as the math allows. A team's division changes from one year to the next depending on where it finished the year before.

Divisions are a scheduling device. They exist because the league needs to carve twenty-four teams into groups that play each other fully, and three groups of eight is the arrangement that makes a seven-game round robin fit inside a ten-game intra-conference schedule. A team plays every other team in its division once, and then plays three games against teams in the other divisions. That is ten games. The model is borrowed in spirit from European club football, where the intuition is that the schedule should be as nearly round-robin as the calendar allows; the Champions Conference cannot quite manage a full twenty-three-game round robin inside a twelve-game regular season, and the snake-drafted divisions are the structural compromise that preserves as much of the round-robin's logic as possible. The compromise also carries a side benefit: because divisions reshuffle annually, no division accumulates permanent identity or permanent grievance. A program is in Division II this year and Division I next year; a division is just the group of seven teams your program happens to play every week this autumn.

The three cross-division games are assigned by a principle the league calls mirrored rotation. Each team's slot within its division — Division I's top-seeded team is Slot 1, its second-seeded team Slot 2, and so on through Slot 8 — determines two of its cross-division opponents directly. Slot 1 in Division I plays Slot 1 in Division II and Slot 1 in Division III. Slot 5 plays Slot 5 and Slot 5. The third cross-division game rotates through remaining opponents on a predetermined cycle that covers every cross-division matchup over roughly five or six seasons. The mirroring produces a specific and deliberate consequence: the best teams in each division play each other every year, and the weakest teams in each division play each other every year, and the same is true at every slot in between. A top-seeded program cannot avoid the top of the other two divisions. A bottom-seeded program cannot avoid the bottom. At every position in the standings, the games that matter for that position are built into the schedule.

The two rivalry games are the part of the schedule each team's home conference fills in. A Champions Conference team remains a member of its home conference for every non-football purpose, and for football it retains two dates on which its conference assigns a rivalry opponent. The games are coordinated by each home conference, which knows better than anyone which matchups matter to its members. The Iron Bowl is scheduled because it is the Iron Bowl. The Egg Bowl is scheduled because it is the Egg Bowl. A conference whose members include one Champions Conference team and one non-Champions Conference team schedules the rivalry as a cross-membership game, and the game happens regardless of which side of the league the rival is on. An edge case: when two teams are Champions Conference rivals and draw each other through the cross-division rotation, they play twice that season, and the second game can be played as a revenge game for the first game's loser. This happens rarely, and when it does, the sport gains a second Ohio State–Michigan in a year that has one. The rules accommodate it rather than forbid it.

What the twelve games produce is a schedule in which every game matters. The seven division games matter because they determine the league's most concentrated competitive picture — a team's performance against the seven opponents it plays a full round robin against. The three cross-division games matter because of what mirrored rotation does with them: a top-seeded program's cross-division games are against other top-seeded programs, and those games move the top of the standings directly. The two rivalry games matter on their own terms, which is why they were preserved. There is no thirteenth game; there is no fourteenth. Every game on the calendar is either intra-league or a preserved rivalry, and a team's record at the end of the season reflects exactly those games, weighted by nothing except how many the team won.

That is the structural point. The standings at the end of a Champions Conference regular season are produced by a set of games that was balanced at the start of the season — division round robins, mirrored cross-divisions, rivalries — and the result is a record that is immediately legible.

## IV. How promotion and relegation work

A closed league is a league whose competitive questions get smaller every year. The programs inside it play for championships; the programs outside it play for something less. Over time, the gap between the two becomes self-reinforcing, because the programs inside get the schedules, the revenue, and the recruiting access that make staying inside easier, while the programs outside get exactly the opposite. The Champions Conference would face this problem acutely. A league of twenty-four teams selected on merit in its first year is a league whose merit case lasts exactly one year; in year two, the case needs to be remade, and the mechanism for remaking it is promotion and relegation.

Four teams change places annually. Two are promoted outright, two are relegated outright, and two additional places are decided by head-to-head games between the bottom of the Champions Conference and the top of the Promotion Playoff. The net effect is four teams out, four teams in — roughly seventeen percent of the league — every year.

Relegation is the simpler half of the mechanism. The twenty-third and twenty-fourth teams in the final Champions Conference standings are relegated automatically. No game to save themselves, no appeal, no tiebreaker involving strength of schedule or signature wins; the league is a closed set of games, the standings are the result of those games, and the bottom two positions carry the consequence the structure assigns them. Those programs return to their home conferences for the following football season and rejoin their conference-mates for a schedule that now includes football as well as every other sport.

The twenty-first and twenty-second teams get a second chance. They play Boundary Games. Their opponents come out of the Promotion Playoff, which is where the other half of the mechanism lives.

The Promotion Playoff is open to the champion of every FBS conference outside the Champions Conference. There are ten such conferences under the conference map the proposal assumes — the six power conferences with programs outside the Champions Conference in any given year, plus four conferences that exist today and would continue to exist under the proposal: the Mountain West, the Sun Belt, Conference USA, and the Mid-American Conference. Every one of those ten champions qualifies. There is no committee, no at-large bid, no selection meeting. If a conference has a champion, the champion is in the playoff.

The ten teams are seeded by a transparent formula based on regular-season performance — winning percentage, strength of schedule, quality of wins, and conference-championship game performance — and the bracket runs single-elimination over three December weekends. The two top seeds receive byes into the quarterfinals; the other eight play a round of play-in games, then quarterfinals, then semifinals. The two semifinal winners are promoted directly to the Champions Conference for the following season. The two semifinal losers survive to play the Boundary Games.

The Boundary Games are the joint where the two systems meet. On the first Saturday in January, at neutral sites, two games are played: Champions Conference twenty-second versus Promotion Playoff third place, and Champions Conference twenty-first versus Promotion Playoff fourth place. Winner earns — or retains — a Champions Conference spot. Loser goes down, or stays down. The games fall on the Saturday between the CFP quarterfinals and semifinals, which is deliberate; the weekend has national attention on college football already, and the Boundary Games give that attention something structurally meaningful to attach to. By the end of that Saturday, the following year's Champions Conference is set.

The mechanism is asymmetric. An automatic promotion is won by a team that advanced twice, against peers of roughly similar standing, in a playoff structure designed around its own conferences. An automatic relegation is earned by a team that finished last or second-to-last across an entire Champions Conference regular season. The playoff route up is a tournament; the route down is a verdict. Between them, the Boundary Games offer a single game — one afternoon, one result — that decides the remaining two cases. The mechanism is neither pure tournament nor pure meritocracy; it is a hybrid that gives each side of the boundary the treatment that matches its structural situation.

One program needs to be named here because its arrangement doesn't fit the structure cleanly: Notre Dame. Notre Dame has been [independent in football since 1887](https://fightingirish.com/1913-the-program-changer/), and since 2014 it has played a partial ACC schedule while remaining outside any conference for football purposes. Under the proposal, Notre Dame joins the ACC fully, for football and every other sport. If Notre Dame is in the Champions Conference, the ACC is its home conference for relegation purposes and its rivalry-game assignments come through the ACC like every other member. Remember that those rivalry games do not have to be ACC opponents. If Notre Dame is outside the Champions Conference in a given year, it competes for the ACC championship and, if it wins, enters the Promotion Playoff like any other conference champion. The rule is simple: access to the Champions Conference pathway requires conference membership, because the pathway runs through conferences. Notre Dame's independence has been a hard-won exception for most of a century; the proposal treats it as an exception the new structure does not preserve. Whether Notre Dame accepts that is a separate question.

Four teams change places annually is a decision that rests on evidence. The Premier League, as the closest available precedent, turns over three of twenty — fifteen percent — each year, and the structure has held for three decades with that rate producing both stable league identity and consequential late-season matches at the bottom of the table. The Champions Conference tunes the rate slightly higher, to about seventeen percent, because the larger candidate pool warrants slightly faster circulation and because American college football tolerates roster change — through graduation, transfer, and recruiting — more readily than a European professional football club does. The number is not sacrosanct. Three teams would be defensible, five would be defensible, and any future spec revision could tune the rate based on what the first several seasons produce. Four is the number the proposal rests on because four is the number that survives the arithmetic: two automatic promotions, two automatic relegations, and two Boundary Games that resolve the marginal cases.

What the annual turnover produces is a league that stays the best twenty-four. Not the best twenty-four of 2024, locked in by the first year's selection and protected from challenge for a decade; the best twenty-four as of this season's end, re-examined every year by the standings themselves. A program that joins the Champions Conference and then fails to compete at Champions Conference level goes down. A program that wins its conference and then wins a December tournament comes up. Over time the league and the conferences that fuel it find balance. The boundary is alive, and the league behind it is legible in the same way its standings are.

## V. The CFP, re-imagined

A postseason's job is to identify the national champion from a field with a legitimate claim to include it. Everything else — the seeding process, the bracket shape, the number of teams, the television distribution — is implementation. The hard question is whether the field is the right field. The current College Football Playoff answers this question through a selection committee that meets for roughly two months each season, publishes weekly rankings, and on the first Sunday in December produces a final twelve-team bracket. The Champions Conference version answers it through standings.

The field simply is the top twelve teams in the Champions Conference standings. Seeds one through four receive first-round byes; seeds five through twelve play first-round games on the campuses of the higher seeds; the bracket runs through the New Year's Six bowls to a national championship in mid-January. The bracket's shape is identical to the CFP format that first ran in 2024. The difference is only in how the bracket gets filled.

What disappears, when the mechanism changes, is worth enumerating. The selection committee disappears. The weekly committee rankings disappear, and with them the cottage industry of explaining and contesting them — the mid-November column about why a two-loss SEC team is ranked ahead of a one-loss Big Ten team, the Tuesday-night television segment dissecting the previous evening's release, the running debate about which set of résumé criteria the committee is weighting this week. The autobid argument disappears — whether the twelfth spot should go to the highest-ranked Group of Five champion or be reallocated to an at-large, an argument that in the 2024 CFP's first year drew enough sustained coverage that [the CFP altered the autobid structure for 2026](https://www.si.com/college/boise-state/football/college-football-playoff-changes-format-for-group-of-six-representation). The conference-championship-as-tiebreaker argument disappears, because conference championships no longer exist for Champions Conference teams. The strength-of-schedule argument disappears, because every Champions Conference team plays a Champions Conference schedule. The eye test disappears. The signature-win argument disappears. The what have you done lately argument disappears. The entire vocabulary the sport has developed over the past decade to litigate its own postseason disappears, because there is nothing left to litigate.

What remains is the standings. The top twelve, by record, are the CFP field. Tiebreakers exist — overall record, then division record, then head-to-head, then point differential — and they resolve every possible edge case inside the mechanism of the league itself, without appeal to external judgment. A team in the playoff is a team that has a better Champions Conference record, or has held a better Champions Conference record under a clearer set of criteria, than the teams that missed. No one argues about it because there is nothing to argue about.

The question this immediately raises is whether a standings-based postseason is legitimate, in the sense that the committee-based postseason attempts to be. The committee's charter is to identify the twelve best teams in college football; the standings' charter is to identify the top twelve finishers in a twenty-four-team league. These are not the same thing, and the proposal depends on making the case that the second one is a better answer to the first question than the committee.

The Champions Conference schedule is built, by design, to be balanced. Every team plays a seven-game division round-robin against a division whose total strength was selected to approximate the other divisions' total strength. Every team plays three cross-division games, two of them against the same slot in each other division — the top of each division playing the top of each other, the bottom playing the bottom. Every team plays two rivalry games chosen by its home conference for their stature. The resulting schedule does not have the wild variance that exists across college football today, where an 11–1 record from one conference is a different object than an 11–1 record from another. A Champions Conference 11–1 is a true 11–1, earned against the same caliber of opponent that every other Champions Conference 11–1 was earned against. The ordinal position in the standings therefore means what it says, and the top twelve is the top twelve not because a committee has certified it as such but because the schedule that produced it was built to produce a ranking that can be read directly.

This also means the bracket is set the moment the final regular-season game ends. No selection show. No committee convening on the first Sunday in December to deliberate over brackets drafted on whiteboards. The regular season ends on a Saturday, the standings are final that evening, and by Sunday morning the CFP bracket is a mechanical consequence of Saturday's results — the top four bye teams, the five-through-twelve first-round pairings, the sites of the first-round games all determined by the seeds. The postseason weekend, under the current arrangement, is organized around the question of who is in. Under the proposal, the question is settled, and the weekend is organized around the question of how the games will go, which is what a postseason weekend is supposed to be organized around.

There is one thing the committee-selected CFP could do that the standings-based format cannot, and honesty requires naming it. A committee can include a team from outside the field based on a judgment call. Every season for the last decade has produced at least one plausible candidate for that judgment. [2023 Florida State](https://www.espn.com/college-football/story/_/id/39034100/undefeated-florida-state-left-college-football-playoff), undefeated in the ACC and left out by the four-team committee the last year before expansion. [2017 UCF](https://www.si.com/college/2018/01/03/ucf-playoff-auburn-peach-bowl-national-championship), undefeated in the American and not seriously considered for the four-team playoff of its era. [2014 TCU and Baylor](https://www.espn.com/college-football/story/_/id/24638416/when-ohio-state-kept-tcu-big-12-2014-playoff), Big 12 co-champions excluded in favor of Ohio State. Each of these was, at the time and in retrospect, a defensible committee decision and also a defensible committee outrage; a reader's priors about which one depend on the reader. Under this proposal, none of those teams gets that consideration. They get the Promotion Playoff, which is a path to next year's Champions Conference rather than this year's CFP. The tradeoff is real. The Promotion Playoff adds structure and legitimacy to an access mechanism that currently does not exist, and it does so at the cost of the committee's flexibility to honor an exceptional single-season case in the season it occurs. The proposal argues that the tradeoff is worth it, because the flexibility has historically been exercised inconsistently and the access mechanism has historically not existed at all. Anyone could disagree in good faith.

What the standings-based format offers in exchange is verifiability. Anyone can read the Champions Conference standings at the end of the regular season. Anyone can see the top twelve. Anyone can verify that the bracket is what the standings produced. The committee version of the CFP has, over its short history, required its participants to trust that thirteen well-credentialed people in a room have arrived at the correct field; the standings version requires its participants to trust only that wins and losses have been counted correctly. It is a smaller request, and the postseason that rests on it is the stronger one.

## VI. What's preserved

The proposal is often read as a radical restructuring, and in mechanical terms it is. It creates a new league. It replaces the postseason's selection mechanism. It introduces promotion and relegation to a sport that has never operated on those terms. But the mechanical radicalism is contained to the parts of college athletics that are currently under the most strain, and the rest of the sport — which is to say, the majority of it — the proposal leaves largely intact. It is worth walking through what stays in some detail, because the case that the proposal is conservative about the things worth being conservative about is central to the argument that the proposal is better than what the sport is heading toward on its current course.

The rivalries stay. All of them. Ohio State and Michigan in The Game. Alabama and Auburn in the Iron Bowl. Ole Miss and Mississippi State in the Egg Bowl. Army and Navy, [a game older than nearly every program in the Champions Conference](https://www.army.mil/article/31068/the_first_army_navy_game) and most of the Group of Five. Oklahoma and Texas in the Red River Rivalry. Florida and Georgia in The World's Largest Outdoor Cocktail Party. Iowa and Iowa State. Oregon and Oregon State. Whatever rivalry a program has — and nearly every FBS program has at least one — the proposal's two-rivalry-game rule preserves it. The rule accommodates in-conference rivalries, cross-conference rivalries, and cross-membership rivalries where one team is in the Champions Conference and the other is not.

This is not sentimentality; it is structural preservation, and the distinction matters. The current arrangement talks about tradition more than it honors it. The Pac-12's dissolution ended, among other things, the USC-UCLA rivalry's meaning as a conference game — the two programs now play each other as Big Ten members, and the game's context has changed whether anyone wanted it to or not. The [Backyard Brawl between West Virginia and Pittsburgh was interrupted for over a decade by realignment](https://www.pittwire.pitt.edu/pittwire/features-articles/pitt-wvu-backyard-brawl-2022) and only restored as a non-conference scheduling courtesy. [Texas and Texas A&M went unplayed from 2011 to 2024](https://www.espn.com/college-football/recap/_/gameId/401628445) because Texas A&M left the Big 12 for the SEC and the schools could not find a non-conference scheduling arrangement. Realignment has been quietly eliminating deep rivalries for fifteen years. The proposal reverses the process. Each Champions Conference program dedicates two schedule slots specifically to its rivalries, and each program's home conference is the entity that selects them — which means the rivalries that matter to programs are the ones that get played, rather than the ones that can be accommodated between television-driven scheduling obligations.

The conferences stay, for every sport that isn't football, and for football in the years a program is outside the Champions Conference. Michigan's volleyball team plays a Big Ten volleyball schedule against opponents within a day's drive — Michigan State, Ohio State, Illinois, Purdue, Indiana, Wisconsin. It does not have to fly to Piscataway or Los Angeles, because the Big Ten is no longer structured around maximizing football revenue, because football revenue no longer drives the Big Ten. Stanford's swim team competes in the Pac-12, reconstituted along its historical lines. The ACC is a conference of schools on and near the Atlantic Coast again. Every non-football sport gets back the conference structure it was built for. The cost of a conference schedule drops. Athletes sleep in their own beds on weeknights. Administrative departments can plan seasons on travel budgets proportional to revenue rather than in excess of it.

The specific conference map the proposal assumes — roughly the arrangement that existed around 2012 — is one choice among several debatable ones, and the number is not critical. The underlying claim is that a map that predates the recent football-revenue distortion is a better starting point than one that is the distortion's direct product. The 2012 version preserves the ACC's absorption of the old Big East's football schools, which made sense on football-quality grounds and has held up since; it undoes the 2021-through-2024 expansions that created the current map. Someone else might draw the line in 2004 or 2014 and be reasonable about it. The important structural fact is that once football revenue no longer drives the conferences, the conferences are free to organize themselves around what their other sports actually need. The 2012 map is the proposal's best guess at what that produces. It is merely a starting point.

The regular-season calendar stays. Saturdays in September and October and November still look like Saturdays in September and October and November. Champions Conference teams play a twelve-game regular season; non-Champions Conference teams play their conference schedules. Rivalry weekend, the final regular-season Saturday, is still rivalry weekend, and under the proposal it becomes more important rather than less — rivalry games are two of the twelve slots on every Champions Conference team's calendar, concentrated in prominent positions, and the weekend that traditionally closes the regular season is the one around which several of them are anchored. The overall cadence of the sport's autumn — the long Saturday of games that begins at noon Eastern and ends with a late night game in Hawai'i, the slow narrowing of the season's questions from dozens of teams to a handful, the week-by-week accumulation of the standings that will determine the postseason — is unchanged. What changes inside the cadence is what the games are for. The cadence itself is preserved.

Conference championships stay for the conferences that still crown them. With this there is a subtle distinction. Champions Conference teams do not play for conference championships; their championship is the College Football Playoff, and the path to the CFP runs through the Champions Conference standings. But every other FBS conference still has a championship game, and under the proposal that championship game takes on a function it currently does not have: the winner enters the Promotion Playoff. A Mountain West championship game is played for a bowl bid, a trophy, and a berth in the ten-team playoff that could put the winner in the Champions Conference next year. The stakes are larger than they are now. The same is true for the Sun Belt championship, the Mid-American championship, the American championship, and any other FBS conference. The championships matter more than they currently do, because they are now the gateway to something greater.

Regional character, which the sport has been losing for fifteen years, comes back. This is partly a consequence of the items above — if the conferences reorganize regionally for everything other than football, and if football in its elite form is separated out into its own structure, then the rest of college athletics returns to the geographic logic it was built on. But it is also its own thing, because regional character is what produces rivalries in the first place. Proximity produces repetition; repetition produces familiarity; familiarity produces the accumulated mutual knowledge that makes a rivalry worth the name. ACC basketball has the rivalries it has because Duke and North Carolina have been playing each other twice a year for a century, and because both schools are a half-hour's drive apart. The sport lost something when the Big East basketball of the 1980s and 1990s fragmented, and it is currently losing more as the remaining conferences dissolve into national constructs. The proposal restores the conditions under which new rivalries can accumulate and old ones can be sustained.

The proposal is structurally radical about what is currently broken, and conservative about what still works. The league is new, the postseason's mechanism is new, the promotion-and-relegation system is new. But the rivalries survive, the conferences come back, the calendar is unchanged, the championships matter more, the regional geography returns, and the ordinary weekly experience of college football in the fall continues to look like what it has looked like for a century. The parts of the sport the current arrangement is under the most pressure to dismantle are exactly the parts the proposal preserves. The parts it changes are the parts that are failing to work inside the current structure. What is left, after the changes and the preservations are accounted for, is a sport that is recognizably the same sport, running on a structure that allows it to evolve.

## VII. What's lost, and why that's acceptable

A proposal that asks for structural change is a proposal that has to account for what the change takes from its participants. What follows is that accounting. It is real — several of the things the current arrangement provides would not survive the transition, and the people who value those things have a legitimate claim to be heard. Some of what follows will read as painful to defenders of the current system, and that is appropriate; a proposal that cost nothing would be a proposal worth nothing.

The first thing that is lost is the selection committee's capacity to honor an exceptional single-season case. A 2017 UCF, undefeated in the American, does not get CFP consideration under the proposal in the season that undefeated run occurs. They get the Promotion Playoff, which is a path to the following year's Champions Conference. This is a real constraint, and it is one the proposal accepts in exchange for the standings-based field's predictability.

The second loss is the conference championship games for the programs that currently play in them. The SEC Championship Game has been, [for most of its thirty-three years, one of the most consequential single games in the college football calendar](https://www.espn.com/college-football/story/_/id/34408233/how-1992-sec-championship-game-altered-college-football-landscape-forever) — in many seasons it has been more consequential than any individual regular-season game, because it concentrates the conference's year into a single evening of football with a national-championship implication attached. The Big Ten Championship Game has, over its shorter history, taken on a similar role. These games have been, collectively, [among the three or four most valuable individual properties in the sport](https://www.sportsmediawatch.com/2024/12/sec-title-game-viewership-lifts-abc-conference-championship-slate-despite-declines/). Under the proposal, the programs that play in them — the SEC's top-tier members, the Big Ten's, the occasional ACC and Big 12 program — may not contest their conferences' championships. Their championship is the College Football Playoff, and the SEC and Big Ten championship games, in the form they currently take, do not exist for them. The conferences continue, and the conferences continue to crown champions from among their non-Champions Conference members, but the specific property of Alabama vs Georgia for the SEC championship, with a CFP berth on the line, is replaced by a bracket the proposal argues is more legitimate. The replacement is a different object and its value accrues to a different audience. A fan whose season culminates, currently, in their conference's championship game gives that up.

The third loss is program identity bound to conference. For most of the sport's history, a Power Four football program's identity has been tied to its conference in a way that matters to fans, alumni, and the program itself. Ohio State football and the Big Ten have been, for a hundred years, objects that share an identity. Under the proposal, they are bifurcated. Ohio State is a Big Ten program for basketball, baseball, volleyball, and every other sport the school sponsors — and for football, in the years the program is outside the Champions Conference. But for football in the years Ohio State is inside the Champions Conference, which is going to be most years, the program's football identity runs through the Champions Conference and not the Big Ten. The Saturday experience of an Ohio State fan whose football allegiance and Big Ten allegiance have been one thing for their entire adult life becomes, under the proposal, two things that run on slightly different tracks. This is not a catastrophic loss — the program is still the program, the fan is still the fan, and the stadium is still the stadium. But it is a loss of coherence, and the proposal does not pretend to restore it.

The fourth loss is realignment as a program-level mobility mechanism. Under the current arrangement, a program that has outgrown its conference — or that calculates its football prospects improve materially by joining a stronger one — can do so. [Oklahoma and Texas moved to the SEC in 2024](https://www.espn.com/college-sports/story/_/id/37493967/oklahoma-texas-reach-deals-big-12-2024-move-sec) because the SEC's revenue and competitive environment were more favorable to their football ambitions than the Big 12's. USC and UCLA moved to the Big Ten for equivalent reasons. Under the proposal, that mobility does not exist. A program improves its football situation by winning more games, not by changing conferences, because the Champions Conference is the destination that matters and the Champions Conference is earned on the field. Merit-based mobility is a better arrangement than conference-shopping, but it is also a real constraint on programs that have, under the current system, been building their next decade around the existing mechanism. An athletic director who believed their program's upward path ran through conference affiliation loses the path they were planning. They gain a different one. The new path is, I believe, a better one, but it is not the same path.

The fifth loss is the CFP-bid stakes attached to every Power Four regular-season game. Under the current system, a Power Four team's regular season carries implicit playoff stakes: any individual loss can be the difference between making the CFP field and missing it, because the field is committee-selected and loss-count-sensitive. Under the proposal, a Champions Conference team's regular season carries standings stakes — a loss moves you within the top twelve, or from the top twelve to the teens, but less commonly from inside the playoff to outside the league. A non-Champions Conference team's regular season carries conference championship stakes that feed the Promotion Playoff. The aggregate stakes across the sport are, if anything, higher than they are now: more games carry direct structural consequence, because the relegation boundary and the Promotion Playoff qualification are both live in ways that currently do not exist. But the particular weight of a Power Four team losing a September game and watching their CFP odds shift is redistributed. Some of it accumulates at the bottom of the Champions Conference standings, where the relegation boundary creates its own stakes; some of it accumulates in the non-Champions Conference regular season, where conference championships now open doors. Some of it is gone. A reader who has enjoyed the particular shape of SEC team loses in September, falls out of playoff picture by October as a narrative arc is going to find that arc redistributed into different shapes. Structurally this is a gain. Narratively, for some audiences, it is a loss.

The sixth and most concrete loss is revenue for programs that fall on the wrong side of the Champions Conference line. This is perhaps where the proposal strains the most, because the mechanics are specific and the implications are serious. Take Mississippi State. Mississippi State has been a member of the Southeastern Conference since 1932 and currently receives approximately a full share of the SEC's football media deal as part of the conference's revenue distribution. Under the current system, Mississippi State's football revenue is meaningful — meaningful enough to sustain a Power Four athletic department with every facility and support structure that entails — regardless of their on-field competitiveness. Under the proposal, Mississippi State in a year they are outside the Champions Conference is in the regional-form SEC, whose football-only revenue is distributed among its non-Champions Conference members and is smaller in absolute terms than the current SEC's all-members-pooled football distribution. Mississippi State's football revenue goes down. Their other-sports revenue, which continues to come through the SEC's non-football media deal, is affected less. Their path to Champions Conference revenue runs through the Promotion Playoff, which requires winning the SEC's conference championship among its non-CC members in a year when most of the SEC's power is elsewhere. The math is, for a program in Mississippi State's position, genuinely harder under the proposal than under the current system.

The spec provides some mitigation for this. The Conference Development Pool, modeled on the NCAA basketball tournament's unit-distribution system, channels a portion of Champions Conference revenue back to the conferences that send teams to the Promotion Playoff, distributed across all the conference's members over a six-year window. This smooths the revenue loss for conferences whose members occasionally qualify for the Promotion Playoff but don't currently produce Champions Conference programs. The mitigation is real. It may not be sufficient, at the specific program level, to replace the current SEC or Big Ten distribution for a program that currently sits in the bottom third of their conference. Mississippi State makes less money under the proposal than they do now, in most plausible years. This is a real cost, borne by real institutions, and the proposal does not hide it.

The argument for why the cost is acceptable is structural: the current arrangement's revenue concentration is itself the force driving the realignment. A structural problem does not resolve if the structure's most valuable feature — the concentration of football revenue in a handful of conferences — is preserved. The proposal distributes football revenue differently because it has to, and the new distribution cost-shifts from the programs currently at the bottom of the Power Four to the programs that make the Champions Conference. Mississippi State loses revenue share. A program like Boise State, if and when they earn Champions Conference membership, gains it. Over time, across all programs and all cycles of promotion and relegation, the aggregate football revenue is distributed on the basis of the Champions Conference's meritocratic membership rather than on the basis of which conferences consolidated first in the 2020s. The proposal's revenue structure is fairer because it is determined by the same mechanism that determines everything else in the proposal — winning games.

The seventh loss is the stability of incumbency, for programs that get relegated. A program that has been a Power Four football institution for fifty years and finds itself relegated after a bad season under the proposal loses something the current system would not have taken from them. They are, after the relegation, outside the Champions Conference. Their immediate revenue falls. Their recruiting environment changes, because recruits who select a program partly for its platform now see a program without that platform. Their path back runs through a December tournament against other conference champions, which is a different competitive structure than the one they have been operating in and is not guaranteed to return them quickly. Incumbency is not nothing, and relegation removes it. A reader who instinctively feels that sports leagues should be stable — that a program with a fifty-year Power Four history should have some protection from a bad season — is responding to something real, and the proposal does not honor it. Relegation is intended to have teeth.

The accounting, taken together, adds up to this. The committee's flexibility is gone. The conference championship games for the top programs are gone. The coherence of program-and-conference identity is reduced. Program-level mobility through realignment is eliminated. The specific narrative shape of the Power Four regular season is redistributed. Revenue for the bottom half of what is currently Power Four football falls. Incumbency is not protected. Each of these is a real cost, borne by specific fans, specific programs, and specific institutions who would have reasonable grounds to oppose the proposal on its specific terms.

The proposal's answer to the accounting is that the costs are directed. Each loss is a loss the current arrangement is producing in a different form anyway — conference championship games that currently matter are being devalued by the CFP expansion; program identity is being bifurcated by realignment; revenue is already concentrating in a way that will continue to concentrate; incumbency is already failing programs that cannot keep up with the revenue arms race. The difference is that the proposal makes the losses explicit and directed, rather than leaving them to happen implicitly and unpredictably. A reader who prefers the implicit version is, legitimately, choosing a different trade. A reader persuaded by the proposal is accepting that the losses are real, that they are directed, and that what is gained in exchange is worth what is given up.

## VIII. Open questions

A design document with no open questions is either a very small design document or a dishonest one. The Champions Conference proposal is a substantial restructuring of a large and institutionally complex sport, and several of its provisions are unsettled. Some are unsettled because they depend on negotiation that the proposal, as a design document, cannot itself conduct. Some are unsettled because the proposal has identified a problem and described a starting point without claiming the starting point is the final answer. What follows is the list of places where the proposal is explicitly incomplete, offered as an invitation to refine rather than as a catalog of weaknesses to hide.

The first and most significant is Notre Dame. The rule is clear: access to the Champions Conference pathway requires conference membership, because the pathway runs through conferences, and Notre Dame's football independence is an exception the new structure does not preserve. The rule is clean on its own terms. Whether it can be implemented is a separate question. Notre Dame has been football-independent since 1887, and that independence is [bound up with an NBC broadcast arrangement](https://www.espn.com/college-football/story/_/id/39828093/independent-status-more-valuable-ever), an institutional identity carefully cultivated over a century, and a level of cultural leverage no other program possesses. The proposal asks Notre Dame to give up the independence in exchange for access to a new league and a standings-based path to a national championship. Notre Dame may not accept the trade. The proposal's position is that the rule applies regardless — access runs through conferences, and a program that chooses not to affiliate with a conference chooses not to compete for the Champions Conference spot. If Notre Dame refuses, they play an independent football schedule outside the new structure, and they cannot enter the Promotion Playoff, and they do not compete for the CFP because the CFP is Champions Conference only. This is a real consequence and the proposal does not soften it. It is also a consequence only Notre Dame can accept or reject, and the proposal's authors cannot negotiate with Notre Dame on the reader's behalf. What the proposal can do is be clear about the rule; what happens next is up to the institution.

The same rule applies to UConn, the only other current FBS football independent. UConn does not command the broadcast arrangements or football-specific leverage that make the Notre Dame question difficult, but the structural requirement is identical: affiliate with a football-sponsoring conference, or forgo access to the Champions Conference pathway. The proposal treats the two programs symmetrically; the negotiation each faces is not.

The second open question is the method for selecting the inaugural twenty-four. The proposal's first Champions Conference season has no prior Champions Conference standings to rank against, so some one-time method is required. The Champions Conference spec names two candidates: the top twenty-four of the final CFP committee ranking from the year before launch, or a one-time selection committee convened for the purpose. Both yield roughly the same twenty-four programs, which is why the choice matters less than it appears to. A program that feels wrongly excluded by either method has, under the proposal, a clear path to correct the exclusion — win your conference, clear the Promotion Playoff, earn the spot. As a reminder a team only needs to make the semifinals of the Promotion Playoff to have an opportunity to join the Champions Conference. The inaugural problem is the only part of the proposal that is not self-correcting on first principles, and it corrects itself inside a few seasons. The specific choice between the two methods is worth settling, and the proposal does not insist on one.

The third open question is the implementation timeline — the transition from the current arrangement to the Champions Conference. The proposal is a design document; it describes an end state. It does not describe a transition plan, because the transition requires decisions the proposal cannot make: how existing conference-media contracts are unwound or allowed to expire, what happens to bowl commitments signed under current rules, what legal structures govern the new entity's founding. Any working version of the proposal needs a transition plan; no transition plan is yet proposed. A reader whose instinct is to ask "when, and in what order" is asking the right question and should expect the right answer to come later in this process.

The fourth open question is the proposal's relationship to the currently unsettled regulatory environment around player compensation. College football's financial relationship with its players has been in active transformation for five years. The NIL framework permits what it previously forbade; the transfer portal operates under rules that have shifted every year; federal court decisions have reshaped the sport's legal terrain repeatedly; the NCAA's authority to set player-facing rules is itself the subject of ongoing litigation. The Champions Conference proposal assumes that some working framework around these questions exists, but it does not specify one. The proposal's mechanisms — the promotion-and-relegation boundary, the competitive-balance snake draft, the revenue distribution — work under most plausible arrangements of player compensation, but they are not neutral to the arrangement. A Champions Conference with a full revenue-share model for players is a different league than one with an NIL-collective model, and either of those is a different league than one operating under whatever framework emerges from the next round of federal legislation. The essay's position is that the structural argument for the Champions Conference does not depend on the player-compensation question being settled one way or another; the proposal adapts to the framework that prevails. But the proposal also does not solve the player-compensation question.

The fifth open question is the specific seeding formula for the Promotion Playoff. The spec proposes a formula — weighting winning percentage, strength of schedule, quality wins, and conference-championship-game performance — and names it explicitly as a starting point rather than a settled answer. The formula's virtue is that it is transparent and tunable; its weakness is that no seeding formula is perfectly neutral, and the ten conferences whose champions enter the Promotion Playoff differ enough in profile that any formula will advantage some conferences' cases over others. The proposal's answer is that the formula's inputs are public and its outputs are verifiable, and that adjustments can be made between seasons as the evidence accumulates. This is among the most tunable provisions in the proposal, and it is probably the one most likely to be refined after launch.

These are the questions the proposal has identified and has not settled. The list is not exhaustive. Any structural proposal at this scale encounters problems in implementation that cannot be anticipated from a design document, and the author expects that readers, reporters, athletic directors, and eventually the institutions the proposal affects will identify open questions the proposal has not yet named. The correct response to an identified problem is to refine the proposal.

## IX. An invitation

What you have just read is one person's structural proposal for a sport that belongs to many. I built it carefully, over an extended period, because I believe the current arrangement is failing in ways that the sport's institutions are not equipped to fix from the inside and because I think a restructured league with transparent membership is the evolution the sport is most likely to survive the next twenty years under. But the proposal is a design document, not a consensus document, and it acquires its usefulness only when other people engage with it on its own terms.

There are three specific things a reader persuaded by the proposal — or usefully skeptical of it — can do.

The first is to write. The commissioner email, commissioner@thetwenty-four.com, is the address at which the proposal's author reads what arrives. Detailed objections are welcome. Refinements to specific mechanisms are welcome. Challenges to the revenue math, the 2012 conference-map assumption, the Notre Dame rule, the Promotion Playoff seeding formula, the competitive-balance snake draft, any of the seven losses accounted for previously — all welcome. The essay argues that identified problems should be refined rather than defended against; that argument applies to the essay itself. An objection that produces a better version of the proposal is a contribution. An objection that produces a correction is a contribution. An objection that produces a rebuttal is also a contribution, because a proposal that has been argued with rigorously is a stronger proposal than one that has only been agreed with. Collaboration and iteration are what make this stronger.

The second is to engage the proposal on its structural merits rather than on whether it could happen. The sport's institutions have many reasons to resist structural change, and a reader whose response is this would never happen is correctly describing the political environment without engaging the design. The proposal's question is whether this is better. Readers in the sport — athletic directors, commissioners, broadcasters, journalists, the people whose opinions shape what the sport's institutions eventually do — have the standing to engage the second question, and the proposal benefits from their engagement whether they agree or not. Readers outside the sport's institutional structure have the standing too, because the sport is the people who watch it, discuss it, and care about its destination as much as it is its institutions. We should be perpetually considering the long arc of any worthwhile institution.

The third is to share the proposal with people who think deeply about fixing things. The essay's persuasive force is in whether the full argument holds together across its chapters, and the test of that is whether a smart reader finishes the essay and shows it to someone else. A proposal that no one talks about is a private document. This one is published for the opposite reason.

A restructured college football with a twenty-four-team Champions Conference and a real path up and down is a better sport than the one currently emerging after a decade of realignment. It preserves what the current arrangement is in the process of dismantling, and it fixes what the current arrangement is failing to address from the inside. The proposal is ambitious. It is not implausible. The sport needs a thoughtful alternative to its current structure. This is one.

I'd love to hear what you think.
