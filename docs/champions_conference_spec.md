# Champions Conference & College Football Restructuring Spec

## Document Purpose

This specification documents the proposed restructuring of college football to address the fundamental problem: football's outsized revenue is warping conference structures in ways that harm all other sports. The solution decouples elite football competition from traditional conference alignments.

---

## Part 1: The Problem

### Current State
- Football generates the vast majority of college athletic revenue
- Revenue pressure has driven conferences to expand nationally (Big Ten: 18 teams coast-to-coast, SEC: 16 teams)
- Non-revenue sports (volleyball, soccer, swimming, etc.) now travel thousands of miles for conference games
- Traditional conference identities and regional rivalries are being destroyed
- The Pac-12 was effectively dismantled in 2023-2024

### Root Cause
Conferences are trying to serve two incompatible masters:
- **Football**: Wants national TV matchups, brand consolidation, maximum revenue
- **All Other Sports**: Wants regional proximity, manageable travel, sustainable budgets

---

## Part 2: The Solution Overview

### Two-Tier System

1. **Champions Conference**: A 24-team super league for elite football programs
   - Competes for College Football Playoff positioning
   - Promotion/relegation system creates mobility
   - Generates premium revenue through guaranteed elite matchups

2. **Regional Conferences**: Traditional conferences return to geographic footprints
   - All sports (including non-Champions Conference football) compete regionally
   - Conference championships remain meaningful
   - Travel costs and athlete burden reduced

---

## Part 3: Champions Conference Structure

### League Composition
- **24 teams** total
- **3 divisions** of 8 teams each
- Divisions are **NOT regional** - based on competitive balance (see Division Structure below)

### Season Structure (12 Games)

| Game Type | Count | Description |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| Division Games | 7 | Full round-robin against all division opponents |
| Cross-Division Games | 3 | Rotating opponents from other two divisions |
| Traditional Rivalry Games | 2 | Preserved matchups with "home" conference rivals |
| **Total** | **12** | |

**Key Implication: No Cupcake Games**

With 10 Champions Conference games + 2 rivalry games, there is ZERO room for "buy games" against FCS or weak G5 opponents. Every Champions Conference team faces a brutal schedule every week. This is a feature, not a bug - it ensures the league's quality and makes every game meaningful.

### Rivalry Game Selection

**Who Determines Rivalry Games:**
- **Home conference determines the 2 rivalry games** for each Champions Conference team
- Conference knows which matchups matter most to fans and programs
- Conference can balance prestige matchups across its membership
- Flexibility built in - some teams have 1 true rival, conference fills the second slot appropriately

**Can Rivalry Games Change?**
- Yes, flexibility is allowed
- Conference can adjust as circumstances change (e.g., new rivalries emerge, logistics shift)
- No rigid multi-year lock-in required

**What If Both Rivals Are in Champions Conference?**

If your traditional rival is also in Champions Conference (e.g., Ohio State and Michigan):
- **Each team uses one of their rivalry slots on each other**
- The game counts as a rivalry game, not a Champions Conference game
- If they also draw each other in cross-division rotation (same mirror slot), they play twice that year
- The second game becomes an extra home conference rivalry game
- This is a feature, not a bug - more marquee matchups fans want to see

**Cross-Conference Rivalries: Allowed**

Rivalry games do NOT have to be within your home conference. This is vital to protect traditional rivalries damaged by mega-conference expansion.

Examples:
- Texas (Big 12) vs Texas A&M (SEC)
- Kansas (Big 12) vs Missouri (SEC)
- USC (Pac-12) vs Notre Dame (ACC)
- Any other historic cross-conference rivalry

Home conference coordinates with the other conference to schedule these protected matchups.

**Example Applications:**

| Team | Home Conf | Rivalry 1 | Rivalry 2 |
|------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Ohio State | Big Ten | Michigan (both in CC) | Penn State or other Big Ten |
| Texas | Big 12 | Oklahoma (both in CC) | Texas A&M (cross-conf with SEC) |
| USC | Pac-12 | UCLA (both in CC) | Notre Dame (cross-conf with ACC) |
| Alabama | SEC | Auburn | Tennessee or LSU |

**Cross-Division Rotation:**
- Each team plays 3 of 16 possible cross-division opponents per year
- Full rotation through all cross-division opponents over ~5-6 years
- Rotation schedule based on standings position (see below)

### Cross-Division Rotation Schedule

**Approach: Mirrored Rotation (Standings-Based)**

Cross-division opponents are assigned based on the team's slot position after the snake draft:

**Mirror Games (2 per year):**
- Each team plays their "mirror" slot from the other two divisions
- Example: Slot 5 in Division A plays Slot 5 from Division B and Slot 5 from Division C
- Creates competitive balance - top teams play top teams, bottom teams play bottom teams

**Rotating Game (1 per year):**
- Third cross-division game rotates through remaining opponents
- Follows predetermined pattern for each slot
- Full cycle through all 14 remaining cross-division opponents over ~5 years

**How It Works:**
| Your Slot | Mirror Games | Rotating Game |
|-----------|--------------|---------------|
| Slot 3 in Div A | Slot 3 Div B, Slot 3 Div C | Cycles through other slots |
| Slot 7 in Div A | Slot 7 Div B, Slot 7 Div C | Cycles through other slots |

**Home/Away Balance:**
- Over any single year: 2-1 or 1-2 home/away split in cross-division games
- Over full rotation cycle: balanced home/away against each opponent

**Known Edge Case: Repeat Matchups**

Because the rotation is based on **slot position** (which changes annually based on standings) rather than team identity, the same two teams may play each other in consecutive years if they finish in the same relative positions.

*Example:*
- Year 1: Ohio State finishes Slot 2, Georgia finishes Slot 5 → Slot 2 plays Slot 5 → they play
- Year 2: Ohio State finishes Slot 2 again, Georgia finishes Slot 5 again → they play again

**Why This Is Acceptable:**
- Based on merit - if teams keep finishing in similar positions, they've earned the rematch
- Creates organic rivalries between consistently competitive programs
- NFL uses similar system (play same-place finishers from other divisions)
- Unlikely to repeat many consecutive years given roster turnover and competitive fluctuation
- If Ohio State and Georgia keep meeting, that's a matchup fans *want* to see

The elegance and competitive balance of the mirrored system outweighs occasional repeat matchups.

### Division Structure

**Preferred Option: Competitive Balance (Snake Draft)**

Divisions are reconstituted annually using a snake draft based on previous season's Champions Conference record:

| Pick | Division A | Division B | Division C |
|------|------------|------------|------------|
| 1 | Team #1 | | |
| 2 | | Team #2 | |
| 3 | | | Team #3 |
| 4 | | | Team #4 |
| 5 | | Team #5 | |
| 6 | Team #6 | | |
| 7 | Team #7 | | |
| 8 | | Team #8 | |
| 9 | | | Team #9 |
| 10 | | | Team #10 |
| 11 | | Team #11 | |
| 12 | Team #12 | | |
| ... | (pattern continues) | | |

**Ranking for Snake Draft:**
- Based on previous season's Champions Conference record
- Tiebreaker: Head-to-head, then point differential
- **Newly promoted teams automatically occupy bottom 4 slots** (positions 21-24)
  - This naturally distributes new teams across divisions
  - New teams face balanced competition immediately

**Why This Works:**
- Each division is roughly equal in strength
- Fair for standings and relegation purposes
- Rivalries emerge organically through repeated high-stakes matchups (like Alabama/Clemson in late 2010s)
- No permanent division identity, but that's acceptable - Champions Conference rivalries are *earned through competition*

**Alternative Option: Tiered Divisions (Performance-Based)**

For consideration, an alternative approach:

- **Division 1**: Teams ranked 1-8 (previous season)
- **Division 2**: Teams ranked 9-16
- **Division 3**: Teams ranked 17-24

*Pros:* Teams always play "at their level," clear hierarchy
*Cons:* Requires internal tier promotion/relegation rules, adds complexity

This option remains available but adds structural complexity. The snake draft approach is preferred for its elegance.

### Standings Determination

**For Relegation Purposes:**
- **Primary**: Overall Champions Conference record (10 games)
- **Tiebreaker 1**: Division record (7 games)
- **Tiebreaker 2**: Head-to-head result
- **Tiebreaker 3**: Point differential (Champions Conference games only)

This creates clear, objective standings without needing strength-of-schedule adjustments since the snake draft ensures relatively balanced divisions.

## Part 4: College Football Playoff Integration

### Fundamental Change: CFP Is Champions Conference Only

**The 12-team College Football Playoff is exclusively for Champions Conference teams.**

- All 12 CFP spots come from the 24 Champions Conference teams
- Exactly half the Champions Conference makes the playoff each year
- Non-Champions Conference teams compete for *promotion*, not the CFP
- The Promotion Playoff is the path to *future* CFP eligibility, not current year

### Why This Works

| Old System Problem | New System Solution |
|-------------------|---------------------|
| G5 teams rarely make CFP | G5 teams can earn Champions Conference promotion, then compete for CFP |
| "Quality wins" debate | Champions Conference schedule guarantees quality opponents |
| Committee subjectivity | Top 12 of 24 from a balanced league with clear standings |
| Meaningless late-season games | Every game matters for CFP positioning AND relegation |

### CFP Selection Process

With 24 teams and objective standings, selection is simple:

- **Top 12 in Champions Conference standings → CFP**
- **Bottom 12 → Bowl games**
- **Bottom 4 (#21-24) → Also face relegation**

**Seeding mirrors standings:**
| Standing | CFP Seed | Round |
|----------|----------|-------|
| #1 | 1 seed | First-round bye |
| #2 | 2 seed | First-round bye |
| #3 | 3 seed | First-round bye |
| #4 | 4 seed | First-round bye |
| #5 | 5 seed | First round |
| #6 | 6 seed | First round |
| #7 | 7 seed | First round |
| #8 | 8 seed | First round |
| #9 | 9 seed | First round |
| #10 | 10 seed | First round |
| #11 | 11 seed | First round |
| #12 | 12 seed | First round |

No committee debates. No "eye test." No controversy. You are what your record says you are.

### No Champions Conference Championship Game

- With snake draft divisions, there's no natural "division winner vs. division winner" matchup
- Overall record determines standings - no need for additional game
- The CFP serves as the championship tournament
- Champions Conference regular season ends late November with everyone else

## Part 5: Promotion & Relegation System

### Annual Turnover
- **4 teams** change places each year (~17% turnover)
- Balances stability with meaningful stakes
- Modeled on Premier League proportions (15% turnover with 3 of 20)

### Relegation (Champions Conference → Regular Conferences)

| Position | Outcome |
|----------|---------|
| #23 | Automatically relegated |
| #24 | Automatically relegated |
| #21 | Plays Boundary Game vs. Promotion Playoff #4 |
| #22 | Plays Boundary Game vs. Promotion Playoff #3 |

### Promotion (Regular Conferences → Champions Conference)

| Position | Outcome |
|----------|---------|
| Promotion Playoff #1 (Semifinal Winner) | Automatically promoted |
| Promotion Playoff #2 (Semifinal Winner) | Automatically promoted |
| Promotion Playoff #3 (Semifinal Loser) | Plays Boundary Game vs. Champions #22 |
| Promotion Playoff #4 (Semifinal Loser) | Plays Boundary Game vs. Champions #21 |

### Boundary Game Details
- Single elimination game
- **Neutral site venue** (rotating host cities or fixed bowl-style locations)
- Winner earns/retains Champions Conference spot
- Loser is relegated/remains in regular conference

### Relegated Team Logistics

**Return Destination:**
- Relegated teams return to their "home" conference immediately
- All other sports are already competing in that conference
- Maintains the core principle: football is decoupled from all-sports conference structure

**Timing:**
- Immediate return, no delay
- Relegated in January → Full conference schedule in September
- Mirrors promotion: earn your spot in January, playing in new league by September

**Conference Scheduling Adjustments:**
- Each conference decides how to handle a returning team
- Options include:
  - Reserved flex slot kept open each year
  - Temporarily expand conference schedule
  - Adjust existing non-conference games to conference games
- No mandated solution - conferences know a team *might* return and can plan accordingly

**Promoted Teams Leaving a Conference:**
- Conference simply has one fewer team in football schedule that year
- Not complicated - they adjust
- Conference knew this was a possibility

**Revenue:**
- Relegated teams receive their full share from the season they participated in
- **No parachute payments** after relegation
- Clean break financially
- Incentivizes fighting to stay - there's no soft landing

## Part 5: Promotion Playoff Structure

### Qualification
- **10 automatic qualifiers**: All non-Champions Conference FBS conference champions
- No committee selection - win your conference, you're in
- Notre Dame handling is an **outstanding issue** (independent status)

### Seeding
- **Merit-based formula** (not committee selection)
- Transparent, publicly trackable inputs
- Formula naturally rewards stronger conferences without assuming it

#### Proposed Seeding Formula
```
Seed Score = 
  (Win % × 40) + 
  (SOS Percentile × 30) + 
  (Ranked Wins × 5 each, max 20) + 
  (Conference Championship MOV, capped at 10)
```

*Note: Formula inputs are a starting point and subject to refinement*

### Bracket Structure (10 Teams)

**Play-In Round (2 games):**
- #9 seed vs. #10 seed
- #7 seed vs. #8 seed

**Quarterfinals (4 games):**
- #1 seed vs. Winner of 9/10
- #4 seed vs. #5 seed
- #3 seed vs. #6 seed
- #2 seed vs. Winner of 7/8

**Semifinals (2 games):**
- Winner of (1 vs. 9/10) vs. Winner of (4 vs. 5)
- Winner of (3 vs. 6) vs. Winner of (2 vs. 7/8)

**Key Insight**: No championship game needed. Both semifinal winners are automatically promoted, so the semifinals ARE the meaningful final round.

**Determining 3rd and 4th Place:**
- Semifinal losers' seeding for Boundary Games based on original seed
- Higher original seed = #3 (plays Champions #22)
- Lower original seed = #4 (plays Champions #21)
- No 3rd place game required (could add for revenue if desired)

### Notre Dame and UConn: A Rule With a Negotiation Attached

The two current FBS football independents — Notre Dame and UConn — are not exceptions to the system; they are programs for which the system's rule is clear but whose implementation involves negotiation. This section documents the rule, explains why it applies identically to both programs, and names the negotiation piece honestly as the piece that remains hard.

**The rule.**

Access to the Champions Conference pathway requires conference membership, because the pathway runs through conferences. A program that wants the opportunity to earn its way into the Champions Conference — via its conference's annual Promotion Playoff berth — must be a full football member of a regular conference. The rule applies identically to Notre Dame and UConn. There is no special-case carve-out for independents.

Operationally: if a program is outside the Champions Conference and wants in, it must win its conference and then win the Promotion Playoff. To win a conference, one must be in a conference.

**Why the rule is clean.**

The proposal's central claim is that merit is determined on the field, and the field is organized into conferences whose champions compete for promotion. Admitting independents into this structure without requiring conference membership would create two paths to the Champions Conference — one meritocratic, one negotiated — and the proposal's design explicitly refuses that bifurcation.

The rule also provides internal consistency with the rest of the proposal's architecture: every program faces the same structural incentives, every program has the same path forward, no program is grandfathered or excepted based on institutional status or historical independence.

**Notre Dame: the rule and its negotiation.**

- **Current state:** Independent in football since 1887; full ACC member for all non-football sports since 2013; plays five football games per season against ACC opponents since 2014; has an NBC broadcast arrangement that was extended through 2029 and that is structurally bound up with the program's continued independence.
- **Under the proposal:** Notre Dame joins the ACC fully for football, with the same rights and obligations as every other ACC member. If in the Champions Conference: the ACC is their home for relegation purposes. If not in the Champions Conference: they compete for the ACC championship and enter the Promotion Playoff like every other ACC program.
- **The negotiation:** Notre Dame has spent over a century building institutional identity around independence; the NBC arrangement depends on the scheduling flexibility independence provides; full conference membership has been historically refused at every prior opportunity. The rule being clean does not make the negotiation easy. Getting Notre Dame to agree requires either significant leverage (the proposal's CFP exclusivity is the leverage) or a structural compromise the design does not currently permit.
- **If Notre Dame refuses:** The consequence is not a special carve-out; it is exclusion from the Champions Conference pathway. Notre Dame plays an independent football schedule outside the new structure, cannot enter the Promotion Playoff, and does not compete for the CFP because the CFP is Champions Conference only. This is a real consequence and the proposal does not soften it. The essay's Chapter VIII names this as an open question whose resolution is not a rule problem but an implementation problem.
- **Candidate rivalry games (if Notre Dame accepts):** Two of USC, Michigan, Stanford, Navy, Purdue. ACC-internal rivalry selection handled through the same home-conference mechanism that governs every other program.

**UConn: symmetric rule, asymmetric stakes.**

- **Current state:** FBS football independent since 2020. The only other FBS football independent besides Notre Dame as of 2025 (following UMass's departure for the MAC).
- **Under the proposal:** Same rule applies. UConn must affiliate with a conference as a full football member to access the Promotion Playoff. Most natural candidates: the American, the MAC, or a reconstituted Big East football arrangement.
- **The negotiation:** Different shape than Notre Dame's. UConn lacks the NBC contract, the century of independent identity, and the leverage that Notre Dame brings to the table. UConn's independence is recent (2020) and pragmatic rather than identity-defining. A conference affiliation is almost certainly less controversial for UConn than for Notre Dame; the asymmetry between the two programs' negotiations is large even though the rule is identical.
- **If UConn refuses:** Same consequence as Notre Dame's refusal — exclusion from the Champions Conference pathway. UConn's smaller profile makes this consequence less publicly costly for the system but equally real for UConn.

**What's resolved and what's open.**

The rule is resolved: full conference membership required for both programs, no special cases. The negotiations are open — not in the sense that the design hasn't decided what to ask for, but in the sense that the design cannot by itself produce the consent that negotiated outcomes require. The essay's Chapter VIII treats this honestly: open questions are not openness about the rule but openness about whether the affected programs will accept what the rule implies.

## Part 8: Revenue Structure

### Revenue Sources

**Champions Conference-Specific Revenue:**
- CC media rights deal (10 premium games per team)
- CFP revenue (all 12 spots are CC teams)
- Boundary Game TV rights
- CC licensing/branding

**Retained by Schools:**
- Gate receipts, local sponsorships
- Individual school merchandise
- Non-football conference revenue sharing

### CC Revenue Distribution: Hybrid Model

**Base Share (Stability Floor):**
- All 24 CC teams receive equal base share
- Ensures financial stability regardless of standings
- Prevents catastrophic swings from single bad season

**Performance Bonus Pool:**

| Achievement | Bonus |
|-------------|-------|
| CFP Qualification (#1-12) | Share of CFP bonus pool |
| CFP Quarterfinal Win | Additional bonus |
| CFP Semifinal Win | Additional bonus |
| CFP Championship Win | Additional bonus |

This rewards performance without creating existential financial stakes beyond relegation itself.

### Traditional Conference Revenue: Football Carve-Out

**CC Teams:**
- **Lose** football portion of traditional conference media deal
- **Retain** full share of all other sports revenue (basketball, baseball, Olympic sports, etc.)
- CC revenue replaces conference football revenue entirely

**Non-CC Conference Members:**
- Split football media revenue among themselves
- Fewer teams = larger individual shares
- Incentive structure rewards staying competitive

**Clean separation:** Football money follows where you play football.

### Promotion & Relegation Financial Impact

**When Promoted:**
- Leave conference's football revenue share
- Gain CC base share + bonus eligibility
- Net impact: Almost certainly positive (CC deal more lucrative)

**When Relegated:**
- Lose CC revenue share immediately
- Rejoin conference football revenue share
- **No parachute payments** - clean break incentivizes fighting to stay

**Promotion Bonus:** None. The CC revenue share itself is the reward.

### Conference Development Pool: "Units" Model

Borrowing from NCAA Tournament basketball distribution:

**How It Works:**
- Portion of CC revenue funds "Conference Development Pool"
- Promotion Playoff participation earns "units" for conferences
- Units accumulate and pay out over 6-year rolling window (like March Madness)

**Unit Values:**

| Achievement | Units Earned |
|-------------|--------------|
| Promotion Playoff Qualifier | 1 |
| Promotion Playoff Quarterfinal Win | 1 |
| Promotion Playoff Semifinal Appearance | 1 |
| Promotion to Champions Conference | 2 |
| Winning Boundary Game | 1 |

**Key Features:**
- Units go to the **conference**, not the individual school
- Conference distributes among all members as they see fit
- Incentivizes conferences to develop multiple competitive programs
- Rising tide lifts all boats within a conference

**Example:**
- Sun Belt's James Madison makes Promotion Playoff
- JMU wins Quarterfinal, loses Semifinal, wins Boundary Game
- Sun Belt earns: 1 (qualifier) + 1 (QF win) + 1 (SF appearance) + 1 (relegation win) = 4 units
- Those units generate revenue distributed to ALL Sun Belt schools

**6-Year Rolling Window:**
- Smooths revenue fluctuations year-to-year
- One great year doesn't create boom/bust cycle
- Conferences can plan long-term

## Part 9: Governance

### Governing Structure: Independent Entity

The Champions Conference operates as a **new, independent organization** purpose-built for this structure.

**Why Independent:**
- Clean slate, not burdened by existing bureaucracy
- Purpose-built for dynamic membership (promotion/relegation)
- Professional management focused solely on CC operations
- NCAA has enough on its plate; conferences are too fragmented

**Structure:**
- **Board of Directors**: Representatives from member schools (ADs or Presidents)
- **Commissioner**: Hired by the Board, runs day-to-day operations
- **Staff**: Professional administrators for scheduling, media, compliance, etc.

### Voting Rights: Equal + Supermajority

**Principle:** If you're in the Champions Conference, you vote. If you're out, you don't.

**Equal Voting:**
- Each of the 24 member schools gets 1 vote
- Simple, democratic, no tenure-weighting or legacy privileges
- Newly promoted teams have full voting rights immediately
- Relegated teams lose voting rights upon relegation

**Supermajority Requirements (2/3 or 3/4):**
- Protects against slim majorities forcing through major changes
- Required for: core structure changes, revenue formula modifications, bylaws amendments
- Simple majority sufficient for routine operational decisions

### Decision Authority

**Commissioner Authority (Operational):**
- Scheduling details and logistics
- TV production coordination
- Day-to-day administration
- Discipline and enforcement
- Public communications

**Board/Membership Vote (Simple Majority):**
- Annual budget approval
- Commissioner hiring/firing
- Routine policy adjustments
- Bowl game partnerships

**Supermajority Required (2/3):**
- Media rights deal approval
- Revenue distribution formula changes
- Schedule format modifications
- Playoff structure changes

**Supermajority Required (3/4):**
- Expansion or contraction of CC size (changing from 24 teams)
- Fundamental changes to promotion/relegation system
- Bylaws amendments
- Dissolution or merger

### NCAA Relationship: Compliant but Independent

**CC operates within NCAA framework:**
- Player eligibility rules
- Academic standards
- Recruiting regulations
- Transfer portal rules
- NIL compliance

**CC operates independently for:**
- Football scheduling
- Media rights negotiations
- Revenue distribution
- Postseason structure (CFP integration)
- Promotion/relegation administration

**Think of CC as:** The most powerful conference in college football, not a replacement for NCAA. Similar to how SEC or Big Ten currently operate, but with even more autonomy over football-specific matters.

### Media Rights Negotiations

**Process:**
1. Commissioner + professional staff negotiate with broadcasters
2. Media Rights Committee (subset of ADs) provides guidance
3. Board reviews and approves final deal

**Contract Considerations:**
- 6-8 year terms balance broadcaster desire for stability with CC need for flexibility
- Renegotiation windows built in for major changes
- Schools do not negotiate individually - collective bargaining only

### Relationship with Traditional Conferences

CC teams remain members of their traditional conferences for all other sports. Clear delineation required:

**Champions Conference Handles:**
- Football scheduling, media rights, postseason
- Football-specific revenue distribution
- Promotion/relegation administration
- Boundary Game logistics

**Traditional Conferences Handle:**
- All other sports (basketball, baseball, Olympic sports, etc.)
- Non-football media rights
- Academic partnerships
- Bowl relationships for non-CC football members
- Conference championship events (non-football)

**Coordination Points:**
- Rivalry game scheduling (CC works with conferences to set dates)
- Calendar alignment (avoid conflicts)
- Relegated team reintegration (conference handles scheduling adjustment)
- Promoted team transition (conference adjusts to one fewer football member)

## Part 10: Outstanding Issues

### Resolved ✓

#### ~~Champions Conference Schedule~~ ✓
- **Decision**: 10 Champions Conference games + 2 traditional rivalry games
- 7 division games (full round-robin) + 3 cross-division (rotating)
- No room for cupcake games - brutal schedules for all 24 teams

#### ~~Champions Conference Division Structure~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Competitive Balance Snake Draft (preferred option)
- Divisions reconstituted annually based on previous season's record
- Newly promoted teams fill bottom 4 slots, naturally distributed across divisions
- Alternative (Tiered Divisions) documented but not preferred

#### ~~Champions Conference Standings~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Overall Champions Conference record (10 games)
- Tiebreakers: Division record → Head-to-head → Point differential

#### ~~Initial Champions Conference Selection~~ ✓
- **Decision**: One-time simple selection, either option yields similar results:
  - **Option A**: Take top 24 from final CFP rankings in year before launch
  - **Option B**: One-time selection committee picks the initial 24
- **Rationale**: This is an inherently subjective, one-time problem. No matter what method is used, teams on the bubble (#22-28) will feel aggrieved. However, the promotion/relegation system is self-correcting - any "wrongly" excluded team can earn promotion within 1-2 years. By year three, no one will remember or care about the initial selection.
- Don't overthink a problem that solves itself after year one.

#### ~~CFP Integration~~ ✓
- **Decision**: The 12-team CFP is exclusively for Champions Conference teams
- Top 12 in Champions Conference standings make the CFP
- Seeding mirrors standings (1-4 get byes, 5-12 play first round)
- No committee selection needed - standings are objective
- Non-Champions Conference teams compete in Promotion Playoff for future CFP eligibility

#### ~~Champions Conference Championship Game~~ ✓
- **Decision**: No championship game needed
- Overall record determines standings
- CFP serves as the championship tournament

#### ~~Calendar Framework~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Detailed calendar finalized using 2025-26 dates as template
- No games on Sundays during NFL season
- Promotion Playoff: Play-In Dec 13, Quarterfinals Dec 19-20, Semifinals Dec 28
- CFP: First Round Dec 19-20, Quarterfinals Dec 31/Jan 1, Semifinals Jan 8-9, Championship Jan 19
- **Boundary Games: January 4 (Saturday)** - neutral site venues
- Bowl eligibility: CC #13-20 eligible for premium bowls; CC #23-24 eligible if 6+ wins (unlikely)

#### ~~Relegated Team Logistics~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Immediate return to home conference
- All other sports already compete in that conference - football simply rejoins
- Each conference decides how to handle scheduling adjustments (flex slot, expanded schedule, etc.)
- Promoted teams leaving: conference simply has one fewer football team that year
- Revenue: Full share from season participated, but NO parachute payments after relegation
- Clean break incentivizes fighting to stay

#### ~~Notre Dame Status~~ ⚠️ (Open Discussion)
- **Recommended**: Force full ACC membership for all sports including football
- Simplifies system, no special rules, consistent with merit-based principles
- **Controversial**: Notre Dame's independence is central to their identity; NBC contract implications
- **Alternative**: If they refuse, they cannot participate in Promotion Playoff - locked out of CFP pathway
- Documented as open discussion point requiring negotiation

#### ~~Cross-Division Rotation Schedule~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Mirrored rotation based on standings position
- 2 "mirror" games: play your same slot from each of the other two divisions
- 1 rotating game: cycles through remaining cross-division opponents over ~5 years
- **Known edge case**: Same teams may play consecutively if they finish in same slots - this is acceptable and merit-based
- Home/away balance maintained over rotation cycle

#### ~~Rivalry Game Selection~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Home conference determines the 2 rivalry games for each Champions Conference team
- Flexibility allowed - conference can adjust as circumstances change
- If rival is also in Champions Conference, each team uses a rivalry slot on each other
- If they also meet via cross-division rotation, they play twice that year (feature, not bug)
- Cross-conference rivalries allowed and encouraged to protect historic matchups damaged by mega-conferences
- Examples: Texas vs Texas A&M (Big 12/SEC), USC vs Notre Dame (Pac-12/ACC)

#### ~~Specific Calendar Dates~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Full calendar mapped to 2025-26 season dates
- Promotion Playoff Play-In: Dec 13 (Sat)
- Promotion Playoff Quarterfinals: Dec 19-20 (Fri-Sat, parallel to CFP First Round)
- Promotion Playoff Semifinals: Dec 28 (Sat)
- Boundary Games: Jan 4 (Sat) at neutral sites
- Bowl eligibility resolved for all CC standings positions

#### ~~Revenue Structure~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Hybrid distribution model
- Base share for all 24 CC teams (stability floor)
- Performance bonus pool for CFP participants and advancement
- CC teams lose football portion of traditional conference share, retain all other sports
- No promotion bonuses - CC revenue share is the reward
- Conference Development Pool using "units" model (like March Madness)
- Units earned by Promotion Playoff performance, distributed to conferences on 6-year rolling window

#### ~~Governance~~ ✓
- **Decision**: Independent entity with professional Commissioner
- Board of Directors from 24 member schools
- Equal voting (1 school = 1 vote), supermajority required for major changes
- Relegated teams lose voting rights immediately; promoted teams gain them immediately
- NCAA-compliant but operationally independent
- Commissioner negotiates media deals, Board approves
- Clear split: CC handles football, traditional conferences handle all other sports

#### ~~Basketball Consideration~~ ✓ (Not Recommended)
- **Decision**: Do not implement a Basketball Champions League
- March Madness already achieves the inclusivity and merit-based outcomes CC creates for football
- See Part 10 for full analysis

## Part 10: Basketball Champions League (Considered, Not Recommended)

### Why Basketball Doesn't Need This

The Champions Conference solves a specific problem: football's outsized revenue warping conference structures and locking out worthy programs from the playoff. Basketball doesn't have this problem.

**March Madness Already Works:**
- 68 teams (expanding to 72)
- Every conference champion gets an automatic bid
- At-large bids reward quality from any conference
- Cinderella stories happen regularly
- No program is locked out if they perform

**Revenue Distribution Already Exists:**
- The "units" model we borrowed for our Conference Development Pool is literally how March Madness works
- Conferences already share in tournament success
- No need to create a new mechanism

**No Playoff Access Problem:**
- A 25-5 mid-major makes the tournament
- A 10-2 G5 football team often doesn't make the CFP
- Basketball's system already rewards merit across all levels

### What It Could Look Like (Hypothetical)

If someone wanted to implement it anyway:

- 24 elite programs form Basketball Champions League
- Round-robin within league (~23 conference games)
- Additional non-conference games
- Top 12 get automatic NCAA Tournament bids
- Bottom 4 face relegation

### Why We Don't Recommend It

| Football Problem | Basketball Status |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| G5/mid-majors locked out of playoff | Mid-majors make tournament regularly ✓ |
| No path to elite competition | Win your conference, you're in ✓ |
| Revenue concentrated at top | Units model already distributes ✓ |
| Meaningless late-season games | Conference tournaments add drama ✓ |
| Committee subjectivity decisive | Exists but less impactful (68 bids vs 12) ✓ |

**The only arguments FOR:**
- Guaranteed elite matchups (Duke-Kentucky-Kansas round-robin annually)
- Simplified TV scheduling
- Relegation drama

**The costs would be:**
- Destroying conference basketball traditions (Big East, ACC, Big 12 are GREAT for basketball)
- Reducing Cinderella opportunities
- Complicating the NCAA Tournament selection
- Solving a problem that doesn't exist

### Conclusion

Basketball's existing structure—conference play plus NCAA Tournament with automatic bids—already achieves the inclusivity and merit-based outcomes that the Champions Conference creates for football. There is no need to disrupt a system that works.

The Champions Conference exists because football broke college athletics. Basketball didn't break anything.

## Part 12: Design Principles

Throughout our discussion, these principles have guided decisions:

1. **Merit over committee selection**: Outcomes determined on the field, not in a room
2. **Transparency**: Formulas and criteria are public and trackable
3. **Stability with stakes**: ~17% turnover balances consistency with meaningful consequences
4. **Preserve traditions**: Rivalry games maintained, conference identities restored
5. **Solve the root problem**: Football revenue separated from all-sports conference structure
6. **Inclusivity with standards**: Clear path for any program to reach the top
